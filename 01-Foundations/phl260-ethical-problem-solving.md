# PHL 260 — Ethical Problem-Solving

Completed: April 2025

## Topics covered
- Ethical frameworks — exploring multiple approaches
  to ethical decision-making including consequentialism,
  deontology, and virtue ethics
- Critical thinking — applying structured reasoning
  to evaluate ethical implications of decisions
- Cultural and global perspectives — examining how
  ethics vary across cultures and global contexts
  and how that affects decision-making
- Professional codes of ethics — applying ethical
  frameworks to professional environments and
  industry standards
- Practical ethical application — evaluating ethical
  implications of decisions in personal, professional,
  and community environments

## What I understand now that I didn't before
- Ethical frameworks are decision-making tools not
  just philosophical concepts. When facing a difficult
  decision with competing interests, having a framework
  — what produces the best outcome, what are my duties,
  what would a person of good character do — provides
  structure to reach a defensible conclusion.
- Ethics in professional environments are not always
  clear cut. Professional codes of ethics provide
  guidance but real situations often involve competing
  obligations and ambiguous boundaries that require
  genuine judgment rather than rule-following.
- Cultural context affects ethical judgment. What is
  considered acceptable disclosure, privacy, or
  authorization varies across legal jurisdictions and
  cultural norms — directly relevant in cybersecurity
  where incidents and investigations frequently cross
  international boundaries.

## Connection to malware analysis
- **Responsible disclosure is an ethical decision.**
  When a malware analyst discovers a zero-day
  vulnerability or an active threat campaign, decisions
  about when and how to disclose that information
  involve genuine ethical tradeoffs between public
  safety, vendor notification timelines, and potential
  for harm if disclosed prematurely.
- **Legal and ethical boundaries around malware samples.**
  Obtaining, storing, and analyzing malware samples
  exists in a complex legal and ethical space. Computer
  fraud laws, terms of service, and professional codes
  of ethics all constrain what analysts can legally
  and ethically do — understanding those boundaries
  is not optional.
- **Rules of engagement in security work.**
  Penetration testing, threat hunting, and incident
  response all require operating within defined ethical
  and legal boundaries. Exceeding authorization —
  even with good intentions — creates legal liability
  and ethical violations. PHL 260 built the framework
  for navigating those boundaries deliberately.
- **Attribution ethics in malware analysis.**
  Attributing malware to a specific threat actor or
  nation state carries significant consequences.
  Premature or incorrect attribution can cause
  diplomatic incidents, misdirect defensive resources,
  and harm innocent parties. Ethical frameworks apply
  directly to how analysts communicate confidence
  levels and avoid overreach in attribution claims.
- **Privacy considerations in forensic analysis.**
  Memory forensics, network traffic analysis, and
  endpoint investigation frequently expose personal
  data belonging to individuals who are not the
  subject of the investigation. Handling that data
  ethically — minimizing collection, protecting
  privacy, and limiting use — is a professional
  obligation that PHL 260 provides the framework for.
